From 721 junction lanes, ATLAS produces:

  • 42 junction lane classes in total

As a result, ATLAS run testing on 17 junction lanes (one lane per selected class).

ISSUES DISCOVERED

Note that when Apollo's bounding box (not 3D model) intersects with any obstacle's bounding box, a collision occurs.
The bounding box is a bit larger than Apollo's 3D model showing in the video (i.e. Apollo's website-based human machine interface).
As a result, collision may trigger even when there is gap between Apollo's 3D model and obstacle's bounding box.

Influencing obstacle disabled
A. wrong yielding actions

When the ego vehicle detects an NPC vehicle, Apollo decides to yield to it and results in a collision. It can cross the junction safely by keeping its current speed or overtaking.

Video Description

At around 16s, the ego vehicle detects and decides to yield to an NPC vehicle even though it can pass through the collision region very soon. Hence, the ego vehicle first stops completely and then restarts its motion (19s). Finally, the ego vehicle and the NPC vehicle cause a collision (25s).
However, if we remove the NPC vehicle, the ego vehicle has the same motion before 16s, but it can pass the collision region smoothly without stops, which can be seen from .

B. wrong overtaking

When the ego vehicle detects an NPC vehicle whose speed is slower, Apollo decides to overtake the NPC vehicle and results in a collision. It is safe if the ego vehicle yields to the NPC vehicle.

Video description

When the ego detects the NPC vehicle moving from the right, it selects to overtake (6s). At 8s, the ego vehicle finds that it cannot pass through the obstacle and decides to yield to the NPC vehicle. However, the current speed is 34 km/h, and the ego vehicle cannot stop itself, resulting in a collision (9s). Since there is a long distance when the ego vehicle detects the NPC vehicle, it could yield to the NPC vehicle if it slowed down its motion.

C. wrong switch from yielding to overtaking

During the collision avoidance, the ego vehicle first yields to the NPC successfully. However, Apollo starts to overtake before the NPC vehicle passes the collision region, resulting in a collision.

Video description

The ego vehicle first yields to NPC vehicle 4 and stop completely (17s). However, the ego vehicle restarts its motion quickly to overtake quickly, even though the NPC vehicle does not pass through the collision region (18s). Hence, a collision occurs (21s). In this case, since the ego vehicle has decided to yield to the NPC vehicle, it should stop to wait for the passing of the NPC vehicle.

D. emergency braking too soft

In some emergencies, for example, when an NPC cuts in, and the ego vehicle cannot overtake since another NPC is ahead, Apollo still generates a gentle brake, rather than emergency braking. If Apollo took complete braking, the ego vehicle would stop before causing a collision.

Video description

The ego vehicle must brake immediately when the NPC vehicles starts their motion at around 2s (3s). However, the vehicle brakes too slightly by increasing the braking force slowly from 0 to a half of the maximal force and then remaining (5s). Thus, a collision occurs (7s). However, if the ego vehicle braked with the maximal braking force, it could avoid collisions.

Influencing obstacle disabled
E. lack of coordinated collision avoidance

Apollo performs collision avoidance with each NPC vehicle independently, so the ego vehicle may cause a deadlock with other vehicles or block the motion of other NPC vehicles.

Video description

First, the ego vehicle detects a vehicle in front, so needs to slow down its motion and follow the obstacle ahead (the green wall) (2s). Then, the ego vehicle detects another vehicle crossing the junction and determines to overtake by accelerating its motion; however, the ego vehicle finally slows down is motion (4s) and results in a collision (5s). In this case, the ego vehicle can pass through the collision region by overtaking and without causing a collision with the vehicle ahead since the vehicle ahead pass through the ego vehicleâ€™s destination during the first 4 seconds. F. decision conflicts

Apollo determines to slow down the ego vehicle to arrive at the destination or follow the vehicle ahead; Meanwhile, Apollo needs to perform overtaking to avoid cut-in collisions. In this case, the ego vehicle finally slows down its motion and causes a crash.

In this video, when the ego vehicle is close to its destination, it should slow down; however, there is also an NPC vehicle approaching, triggering an overtaking action (8s). Finally, the ego vehicle decides to slow down its speed (11s) and cause a collision (13s). However, in such a case, safety is the most important and has the highest priority, the ego vehicle is expected to accelerate its motion to avoid collisions, as shown in .

G. incorrect Prediction

Apollo sometimes cannot predict the motion of NPC vehicles accurately, resulting in no sufficient distance to take safe actions. For example, due to the short prediction duration or prediction of low-speed vehicles as static ones, Apollo cannot detect collision in time, resulting in a short distance to take collision avoidance actions.

Video description

In this video, to avoid collisions with NCP vehicle 1 and 2, the ego vehicle stops its motion first (20s). When these two vehicles have been crossed, the ego vehicle cannot predict the motion of vehicle 5 and re-starts its motion (21s), resulting a collision (23s). Clearly, in this case, the ego vehicle should still wait for the passing of vehicle 5.

H. obstacle Failure

the simulator cannot simulate NPC vehicles' motion after an accident. When two NPC vehicles cause a collision, they may skid everywhere disorderly. Hence, they may block, even collide with, the motion of the ego vehicle. However, this should not be the responsibility of Apollo.

Video description

In the video, obstacle 2 had an collision with another obstacle in the junction after the test started. This flipped the obstacle 2's 3D model over to an upside down posture. This made obstacle 2 to skid recklessly and collide with the approaching Apollo.
